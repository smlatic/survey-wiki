# Chapter 11: IT Basics for the Offshore Professional

## Computers, Networks & Wi-Fi That Won't Quit

You don't need to be a full-on IT guru, but a solid grasp of basic computer and network concepts can save you (and your colleagues) a lot of headaches. Understanding the difference between LAN (Local Area Network) and WAN (Wide Area Network), knowing how to troubleshoot a flaky Wi-Fi connection, or handling a minor software crash: all these skills keep you productive and calm when problems arise.

As a trainee, you'll likely start with simple fixes:

- **Rebooting Hardware:** Sometimes turning the system off and on really does fix the issue.
- **Checking Cable Connections:** A loose Ethernet or USB cable can make it seem like the world is ending, until you plug it back in properly.
- **Verifying IP Addresses:** If you can't connect to a device or server, confirm you're on the correct subnet and IP range.

Major decisions (like choosing the vessel's network architecture or implementing advanced cybersecurity measures) fall to IT specialists and management. But your willingness to learn and solve smaller problems can make you a valuable team member in no time.

## Managing Software & Updates

While it might not seem thrilling, staying on top of software updates is key to smooth operations. Survey applications often evolve rapidly, introducing new features or fixing bugs that could affect your data quality or workflow. Although senior staff typically schedule major upgrades and test new versions before rolling them out, you can still help by:

- **Reporting Glitches Promptly:** If you notice odd behavior (like a button that doesn't work or a sudden crash) tell someone. Early reporting can prevent others from losing work.
- **Tracking Version Numbers:** Keep a small notebook or digital record of which versions of key software are installed. It's surprisingly easy to forget whether the echo sounder software got updated last month or last year.
- **Notifying the Team of Minor Issues:** Even small hiccups (like a misaligned GUI) can be clues to bigger compatibility problems. A quick screenshot and note can help senior staff determine if it's a one-off issue or a sign of something bigger.

Over time, you'll learn which updates are routine and which ones need careful coordination (like major OS changes). By staying aware, you might become the go-to person for version-related questions, making you an invaluable resource for your team.

## Cybersecurity 101: No Phishing, Just Real Fish in the Ocean

Phishing attacks, malware, ransomware: these threats are no joke. They can compromise sensitive project data in a heartbeat. As a trainee, you're not expected to deploy firewalls or develop the company's security policy, but you do play a pivotal role by:

- **Avoiding Suspicious Emails:** If an email looks off (contains weird grammar, unexpected attachments, or alarming links) consult your supervisor or IT.
- **Never Sharing Passwords:** Everyone's busy, and it's tempting to give a colleague your login "just this once," but that undermines security protocols.
- **Reporting Anything Odd:** Whether it's a strange pop-up on your screen or a suspicious external drive you found, speak up. Small anomalies can be early signs of larger security risks.

Your company's reputation depends on safeguarding data, and a single misstep can cost millions or tarnish relationships with clients. Even the smallest security-conscious steps (like logging out of your workstation when you step away) keep the entire project safer.

## Troubleshooting Common Tech Issues

Technology hiccups are inevitable. That's why a calm approach and a few basic troubleshooting tricks can make all the difference:

1. **Restart Software:** Often, simply closing and reopening the application clears temporary glitches.
2. **Check Cable Connections:** Jiggling a loose connector or swapping a questionable cable can restore functionality in seconds.
3. **Verify Licenses and Resources:** Some survey applications require valid licenses or might run slowly if your CPU or RAM is maxed out. A quick look at your Task Manager (on Windows) or Activity Monitor (on macOS) can reveal if a program is hogging resources.
4. **Close Unneeded Tasks:** If your PC starts lagging during a survey, check your running processes. Shutting down unessential apps can free up memory and CPU power.
5. **Document the Steps You Took:** Write down what you tried, even if it didn't work. This helps the next person (often the IT expert) pick up where you left off without duplicating efforts.

If these steps don't solve the issue, or if the problem is more complex (like a network outage or corrupted data) call in the experts. Meanwhile, stay calm, follow standard procedures, and try to learn from the situation. Over time, you'll build a strong troubleshooting intuition that will benefit you whether you're onshore or at sea.
