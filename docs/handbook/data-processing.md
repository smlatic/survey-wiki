# Chapter 8: Data Processing Magic

## From Raw Data to Refined Results

You've collected gigabytes of data: ping after ping of bathymetry, side scan sonar imagery, and sub-bottom profiles. But at this stage, it might look like a messy collection of numbers and fuzzy images, more abstract art than actionable information. Enter the data processors, software specialists, and yes, even you (in time), working together to transform all that raw input into polished charts, maps, and 3D models.

As a trainee, your role might start small. You might help organize files, back them up, or check that the data streams are recording properly. Over time, as you gain experience and training, you'll learn to use software tools to filter out noise, apply corrections, and align datasets. While top-level decisions (like what processing standards to use or how to format deliverables for the client) are made by senior staff and project leads, the hands-on skills you develop here set the foundation for your growth.

## Software Overview: Qinsy, Qimera, SIS, and More

If sonar and sensors are your underwater eyes and ears, processing software is the brain that interprets what those "eyes and ears" are telling you. Different tools serve different purposes:

- **Qinsy & Qimera:** Widely used for acquisition and processing, turning raw bathymetry into crisp surfaces. Qinsy handles real-time data acquisition, while Qimera is its companion for post-processing and visualization.
- **SIS (Seafloor Information System):** Often paired with Kongsberg multibeam systems, providing real-time visualization and data quality checks.
- **GIS (Geographic Information Systems):** Software like ArcGIS or QGIS helps you layer and analyze spatial data, creating maps that combine survey results with other geographical info.

Don't worry if these names sound alien. At first, you'll just observe others using these programs. Over time, you'll become more comfortable navigating menus, adjusting parameters, and understanding why your senior colleagues choose one software for a certain task. Larger decisions (like selecting which software suite to invest in) are often made by project managers and technical leads after consulting the team.

### Survey Manager in Qinsy

One tool you'll encounter early on is Qinsy's **Survey Manager**. This is the project setup module where you define your vessel, sensors, offsets, and coordinate systems. It's where you configure everything before data acquisition begins. Think of it as the blueprint for the survey: you tell the software what equipment you have, where it sits on the vessel (those offsets again), and what coordinate system the project uses. As a trainee, you'll likely see senior surveyors setting this up during mobilization. Pay attention, because understanding Survey Manager means understanding how all your equipment connects to the data.

## Creating Charts and 3D Visualizations

Data processing isn't just about cleaning up noise; it's about telling a story. After all, your final deliverables might be presented to clients who aren't surveyors by trade. They want clear, understandable visuals: contour maps, shaded relief images, or 3D perspectives of the seabed.

Think of it as graphic design for the underwater world. You'll learn to color-code depths, highlight interesting features, and maybe even produce fly-through animations. While the choice of which visualization techniques to use might involve input from clients, senior surveyors, and the project manager, you can contribute by suggesting improvements or learning the shortcuts to turn raw data into something visually captivating.

!!! note "A Note on Online vs Offline Work"
    Data processing and visualization are typically handled by the offline team or during post-survey phases. As a trainee, you'll see this more in the later stages of a project. The online team focuses on real-time acquisition and QC, while the offline team takes that raw data and refines it into deliverables. Both roles are essential, and understanding the handoff between them is key to smooth operations.

## CAD, GIS, and Beyond: Turning Numbers into Narratives

Sometimes, clients need more than a pretty picture. They need detailed plans, engineering drawings, or spatial analyses. That's where CAD (Computer-Aided Design) and GIS step in. CAD tools help produce precise drawings of structures, routes, or layouts, while GIS lets you analyze spatial relationships (like how close a pipeline is to a protected reef).

As a trainee, you might start by learning basic GIS functions (like layering data or adding labels) before diving into complex spatial queries. Experienced team members will guide you. When complex decisions arise, like selecting a coordinate system for final deliverables or applying specialized spatial analyses, you'll see project leads consult with GIS specialists or engineers before finalizing the approach.
