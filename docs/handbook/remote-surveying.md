# Chapter 10: Remote Surveying

## The Rise of the Remote Surveyor

The idea of running an offshore survey from a cozy onshore office (or even your living room) might have sounded like science fiction a few years ago, but it's quickly becoming part of mainstream operations. With reliable high-speed data links, secure cloud storage, and advanced remote-access software, survey teams can monitor and control aspects of a job without being physically aboard the vessel.

### What We Gain

- **Cost & Efficiency:** Having part of the team onshore can lower travel expenses and reduce downtime. If you can troubleshoot equipment or process data in near-real time from a land-based office, you're cutting out the need to wait for the next crew change or send additional specialists offshore.
- **24/7 Collaboration:** Remote staff often work in different time zones, so you can keep data processing going around the clock. When the offshore crew finishes a shift, the onshore team can pick up right where they left off.
- **Safer Conditions:** Fewer people physically on the vessel can mean reduced exposure to harsh weather or potential safety hazards.
- **Environmental Benefits:** Fewer crew flights and boat transfers means a lower carbon footprint. Remote operations reduce the need for helicopter or vessel-based personnel transfers, aligning with the industry's growing focus on sustainability.

### What We Lose

- **Tangible Vessel Experience:** Part of the traditional offshore survey role is feeling the motion of the vessel and experiencing the environment firsthand. When you're onshore, you can't judge sea conditions by simply stepping onto the deck, and you won't have those spontaneous, face-to-face chats that often lead to creative problem-solving.
- **Connectivity Dependency:** If the satellite link goes down, so does your ability to monitor or assist. Redundancy is important, but not all vessels have backup systems in place.

### Still a Team Sport

Remote surveying doesn't replace the offshore crew; it complements them. Critical decisions (like adjusting the survey plan due to unexpected weather) still come from the vessel's leadership, often in consultation with onshore leads. Meanwhile, remote staff handle tasks such as data processing, troubleshooting sensor issues, and offering analytical support in near real-time. This division of labor can save costs and free up offshore personnel to focus on immediate operational needs, without leaving you feeling cut off from the action.

As a trainee, the concept of "dry land surveying" might seem counterintuitive, but you'll soon see that it's just another way of collaborating. The vessel crew and the onshore office are part of the same team, just spread across different spaces and time zones. If you ever miss the salt spray and deck banter, don't worry. Offshore jobs won't be vanishing anytime soon. Instead, remote surveying is simply adding more flexibility to how we get things done.

## Virtual Machines & Network Drives: Setting Up Your Remote Office

Imagine walking into a dedicated Remote Operations Center (ROC), where large monitors display live vessel positions, sensor readouts, and real-time communications, all without physically being on the ship. Modern technology such as virtual machines (VMs) and network drives make this possible. You can run specialized survey applications on powerful remote servers, while cloud storage keeps your data backed up and accessible around the globe.

### What a Remote Operations Center Might Look Like

- **Centralized Monitoring:** A few large screens (or even a video wall) show vessel status, ROV camera feeds, and sensor dashboards in real time.
- **Team Collaboration:** Operators, data processors, and supervisors can be seated side-by-side or connected through secure networks, able to share files, chat, and make calls instantly.
- **High-Speed Connectivity:** Dedicated fiber lines or satellite uplinks ensure low-latency access to vessel systems.

### Your Role as a Trainee

- **Learning Remote Platforms:** You might begin by learning how to log into a VM (Virtual Machine) to access survey software like Qinsy or SIS. This involves understanding VPNs, secure protocols, and user credentials.
- **Managing Data:** You'll likely help organize or retrieve datasets from network drives, making sure files are labeled correctly and stored in the right project folders.
- **Following Guidelines:** Senior IT staff usually decide which cloud provider to use or how robust a VM setup needs to be. Your job is to follow security protocols and ask questions if something seems off.
- **Observing Real-Time Ops:** If your company has a dedicated ROC, you might sit in during a live operation. Watching the onshore team coordinate with offshore personnel can teach you a lot about how to troubleshoot issues and maintain smooth communication.

## Secure File Transfers and Cloud Services

Offshore teams frequently use virtual machines (VMs) hosted in the cloud to run demanding survey software. This lets you log in from a remote location while still leveraging powerful server hardware. Large data files (such as bathymetric grids or ROV video) may also be stored in cloud drives to ensure redundancy and facilitate collaboration. When your vessel has a decent internet connection, you can upload or download the latest survey data without clogging local storage.

### Bandwidth and Satellite Links

Historically, transferring huge files offshore meant dealing with painfully slow satellite links. Nowadays, newer solutions like Starlink can bring high-speed, low-latency internet to remote vessels. While it's not yet universal, Starlink and similar technologies drastically reduce transfer times for large files, enabling near real-time processing and troubleshooting from onshore. However, bandwidth can still fluctuate depending on your location and satellite coverage, so being mindful of data sizes and transfer schedules remains important.

As you progress, you'll learn how to manage these file transfers effectively and securely. You might coordinate with IT staff to schedule large uploads during off-peak hours, or you may become the "go-to" person for organizing folder structures and naming conventions on the cloud server. Demonstrating you can handle data responsibly shows your attention to detail, something managers look for when considering who's ready for more advanced roles.

## Communication Tools & Virtual Collaboration

Modern collaboration tools (video conferencing, instant messaging, shared dashboards) make it possible for distributed teams to work together seamlessly. The key difference to understand is where different roles typically sit:

- **Online surveyors** managing real-time operations are more likely to be in a Remote Operations Center (ROC) or physically offshore. This setup ensures they're connected to the vessel's live data streams and can communicate immediately with the Party Chief or ROV team.
- **Offline processors** working on data after acquisition have more flexibility and may work from a dedicated office or, in some cases, from home for tasks like cleaning up point clouds or generating preliminary maps.

Regardless of where you're seated, clear and proactive communication remains crucial. As a trainee, if something feels off, ask questions. If you need help, say so. Just like on the vessel, the best way to avoid misunderstandings is to speak up early and often.
