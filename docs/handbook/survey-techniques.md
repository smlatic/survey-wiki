# Chapter 7: Survey Techniques and Workflows

## Planning the Survey

A survey without a plan wastes time, burns through resources, and produces unreliable data. Every operation starts with structured planning, and the more you understand this process now, the better you'll be at running one later.

A typical survey plan covers:

- **Scope and Objectives:** What are we trying to determine? Route mapping, pipeline inspection, environmental assessment -- the objective drives everything else.
- **Equipment Selection:** Based on project requirements, the senior team selects the right sensor package. MBES for bathymetry, SSS for seabed imaging, or a combination depending on the deliverables.
- **Line Planning:** Survey lines are laid out to ensure complete coverage with appropriate overlap. Lines are spaced based on swath width, water depth, and required data density.
- **Timing and Weather Windows:** Operations depend on favorable conditions. If a storm is approaching, certain tasks may need to wait.
- **Coordination:** Bridge, deck crew, ROV team, and surveyors all need to be aligned. Shift changes, vessel repositioning, and equipment deployment all require clear communication.

As a trainee, pay attention to how these steps unfold. A well-coordinated plan reduces downtime, saves money, and produces reliable data. Understanding why plans are structured the way they are prepares you for the day you're the one writing them.

## Weather, Vessel Motion, and Operational Limits

The ocean doesn't care about your schedule. Weather changes everything -- vessel motion (pitch, roll, heave) directly affects data quality, and MRUs and INS can only compensate so far.

**How weather affects the work:** High winds make station-keeping difficult, reduce ROV operational visibility, and increase vessel motion. Swells endanger over-the-side equipment. Heavy rain can interfere with radio signals and create hazardous deck conditions. In extreme cases, operations are suspended for safety.

**Staying flexible:** When rough weather is forecast, the senior team may rearrange the schedule -- switching to tasks less affected by motion, like data processing or equipment maintenance. Sometimes waiting out bad weather or moving to sheltered waters is the smartest call. No dataset is worth damaged equipment or injured crew.

As a trainee, watch how these decisions get made. Understanding why the team adjusts plans in response to conditions trains you to anticipate problems rather than react to them.

## Route Surveys

Route surveys are among the most common offshore survey types. They map and assess a planned path for cables, pipelines, or other subsea infrastructure.

Most route surveys combine MBES (for detailed bathymetry) with SSS (to identify potential hazards on the seabed). Magnetometers detect buried ferrous objects, and sub-bottom profilers confirm what the seabed is made of and whether it can support the planned installation.

Even if route surveys aren't your daily task, watching how a team conducts one shows you how multiple sensors and systems work together toward a single objective. A well-executed route survey is good offshore practice in action: gather the right data, interpret it correctly, and set the project up for success.

## Environmental and Geotechnical Surveys

Not all surveys are about infrastructure. Environmental surveys assess seabed habitats, sediment composition, and marine life. They're especially critical near protected areas or sensitive ecosystems. Geotechnical surveys go deeper, analyzing whether the seabed can support the weight and stress of planned structures.

### Environmental Surveys

Baseline studies typically precede major offshore projects -- wind farms, platforms, cable installations. Operators sample sediments, map benthic habitats, and check water quality to confirm the project won't damage sensitive zones or disrupt critical biological cycles. Monitoring continues after construction too, with ROVs or divers checking whether habitats and species are coping with the new structures.

### Geotechnical Investigations

Before building anything on the seabed, you need to know what it's made of. Geotechnical surveys use tools like cone penetration tests (CPTs), vibrocoring, or box coring to physically sample the soil. The data tells engineers whether the seabed can support a foundation, how deep you need to go for stable footing, and what risks (slope instability, shallow gas) might exist below the surface.

### More Survey Examples

- **Dredging and Coastal Works:** Environmental and geotechnical teams collaborate when dredging near coastlines, ensuring the sediment type is suitable for removal and that local habitats won't be harmed.
- **Cable/Pipeline Burial Studies:** Burial protects cables and pipelines from trawling and currents. Environmental surveys check for sensitive species, while geotechnical results confirm whether the seabed can be trenched.
- **Seafloor Mining and Resource Exploration:** Environmental checks ensure extraction won't cause irreversible harm. Geotechnical surveys determine whether the resource is extractable and how challenging the operation will be.

Environmental and geotechnical surveys aren't just regulatory checkboxes. They inform real decisions -- from choosing a turbine location that won't fail in loose sediment to timing projects around whale migration. Seeing how these studies fit into the broader picture reinforces why thorough, well-planned data collection matters.
