# Chapter 7: Survey Techniques & Workflows

## Planning the Perfect Survey: It's Like Baking a Cake, Only Wetter

Ever try baking a cake without a recipe? You'd likely end up with a confusing mess. Survey planning works the same way: without a solid plan, you'll waste time, burn through resources, and produce data that's about as useful as a soggy cake.

A typical survey plan includes:

- **Scope and Objectives:** What exactly are we trying to find out? Are we mapping a route, inspecting a pipeline, or studying the environment?
- **Equipment Selection:** Based on the project's requirements, the senior team picks the right sensors and tools. Maybe it's MBES for bathymetry, SSS for seabed imaging, or a combination of everything.
- **Line Planning:** Survey lines (the paths the vessel will follow) are laid out to ensure complete coverage. Think of mowing a lawn in neat rows, except the lawn is the ocean floor and the mower costs a few million.
- **Timing and Weather Windows:** Operations depend on favorable conditions. If a storm is rolling in, certain tasks may need to wait.
- **Coordination:** The bridge, the deck crew, the ROV team, and the surveyors all need to be on the same page. If shifts change or the ship needs to reposition, it's the captain's call.

As a trainee, pay attention to how these steps unfold. While you're not steering the project yourself right now, seeing how the plans are formed gives you a deeper understanding of why it's so important to get them right. A well-coordinated plan cuts down on panic moments, saves money, and ensures the data you collect is reliable. One day, you might be the one mapping it all out, so the more you learn now, the better prepared you'll be.

## Navigating Weather, Vessel Motion & Other Things You Can't Control

The ocean doesn't care about your deadlines. Weather can turn a calm survey into something like a waltz on a trampoline, and vessel motion (pitch, roll, and heave) can seriously affect your data if you don't compensate for it. That's why MRUs (Motion Reference Units) and INS (Inertial Navigation Systems) are essential, but even advanced technology can only do so much when nature decides to throw a party.

**How weather affects the work:** High winds make it hard to keep the vessel on course, reduce visibility for ROV operations, and whip up larger waves that increase roll and pitch. Swells can endanger over-the-side equipment, and heavy rain can interfere with radio signals or make the deck slick. In extreme conditions, the survey might be postponed for safety.

**Staying flexible:** If rough seas are on the way, the senior team may rearrange the schedule or switch focus to tasks less affected by motion, such as data processing or equipment checks done in calmer conditions. As a trainee, watch closely. Understanding the "why" behind these adjustments trains you to anticipate challenges instead of being caught off guard. Sometimes, waiting out bad weather or moving to sheltered waters is the smartest option. After all, no chart is worth a sprained ankle or a broken sensor.

## Route Surveys: Following the Line

One of the most common survey types you'll encounter is a route survey. These are done to map and assess a planned path for cables, pipelines, or other subsea infrastructure.

Most route surveys combine MBES (for detailed bathymetry) and SSS (to spot potential hazards). Magnetometers might reveal buried metal objects, and sub-bottom profilers confirm the seabed can handle the load. Even if route surveys aren't your daily gig, watching how a team conducts them shows you how various sensors come together to avoid unpleasant surprises.

As a trainee, you won't be the one drawing the final line on a map yet. But by seeing how experts select tools, plan the workflow, and review the final QC, you'll learn the art of seamless operations. A well-executed route survey is the essence of good offshore practice: gather the right data, interpret it intelligently, and set your operation up for long-term success.

## Environmental & Geotechnical Surveys: Science Under the Sea

Not all surveys revolve around placing pipelines or drilling wells. Environmental surveys assess seabed habitats, sediment composition, and marine life. They're especially crucial near protected areas or fragile ecosystems. No one wants to unknowingly damage a coral reef or disrupt a fish nursery. Geotechnical surveys dive deeper, analyzing whether the seabed can handle the weight or stress of whatever you plan to build.

### Environmental Surveys: Beyond Just a Green Checklist

Baseline studies often come before big offshore projects like wind farms, platforms, or cable installations. Operators may sample sediments, map benthic habitats, and check water quality, ensuring they don't accidentally wreck sensitive zones or disturb critical breeding seasons. Monitoring continues after construction, too. Divers or ROVs might check if coral reefs remain healthy, or whether local species are coping well with the new structures.

### Geotechnical Investigations

Before you build anything on the seabed, you need to know what it's made of. Geotechnical surveys use tools like cone penetration tests (CPTs), vibrocoring, or box coring to physically sample the soil. This data tells engineers whether the seabed can support a foundation, how deep you need to go for stable footing, and what risks (like slope instability or shallow gas) might lurk below.

### More Survey Examples

- **Dredging & Coastal Works:** Environmental and geotechnical teams often collaborate when dredging near coastlines, ensuring the sediment type is suitable for removal and that local habitats won't be harmed.
- **Cable/Pipeline Burial Studies:** Avoiding damage from trawling or currents often means burying cables or pipelines. Environmental surveys check for sensitive species, while geotechnical results confirm whether the seabed can be easily trenched.
- **Seafloor Mining & Resource Exploration:** When searching for minerals or aggregates, environmental checks ensure you won't irreversibly harm marine ecosystems. Geotechnical surveys tell you if the resource is extractable and how challenging it might be.

Environmental and geotechnical surveys aren't just about ticking regulatory boxes. They guide your decisions, from choosing a turbine location that won't topple over in loose sediment to timing projects so you don't interfere with whale migrations. As a trainee, seeing how these studies fit into the broader picture underscores why thorough, well-planned data collection is key.
