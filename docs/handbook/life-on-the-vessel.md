# Chapter 2: Life on the Vessel

## Vessel Terminology

Offshore vessels have their own language. Knowing these terms keeps you safe and lets you follow instructions without hesitation.

- **Bow:** The front of the vessel. Mnemonic: it leads the way.
- **Stern:** The rear. If you can feel the engine vibrations, you're near the stern.
- **Port:** The left side when facing forward. "Port" and "left" both have four letters.
- **Starboard:** The right side when facing forward.
- **Aft:** Toward the stern.
- **Bridge:** The vessel's command center -- navigation and overall control happen here. You'll hear radio calls like "Bridge, Survey here..."
- **Deck:** The floor. Calling it "the floor" marks you as new.
- **Galley:** The kitchen and dining area, sometimes called the mess.
- **Cabin/Berth:** Your sleeping quarters. Small, but home for the duration.
- **Bulkhead:** A wall. "Check the bulkhead" means check the wall.
- **Head:** The bathroom. On a ship, you ask for the head.
- **Mess Hall:** The dining and seating area, sometimes used interchangeably with galley.
- **Muster Station:** Your designated meeting point during drills or emergencies.

Common phrases:

- **"Secure It":** Tie it down or lock it in place. Things move at sea.
- **"Heads Up":** A warning or a way to get attention for an announcement.
- **"Copy That":** "I heard and understood."

These aren't just jargon -- they're how the vessel communicates. When someone says "move that gear to the port side, then head to the galley," you need to know what that means without thinking about it.

### Online vs Offline

On a survey vessel, "online" and "offline" don't refer to internet connectivity. They describe two distinct roles in the survey workflow:

- **Online team:** The surveyors managing live, real-time operations. If you're online, you're monitoring positioning, running data acquisition, managing equipment during active survey work, and communicating directly with the bridge and ROV team. You're keeping the live data flowing and verifying it as it comes in.

- **Offline team:** Data processors, geophysicists, video reviewers, and other specialists who work on data after acquisition. They clean, process, QC, and prepare deliverables. The offline team may be working simultaneously on the vessel or remotely from shore.

As a new surveyor, you'll likely start online -- watching and assisting with live operations. It's the fastest way to learn how everything connects.

## Communication Protocols

Offshore vessels are noisy environments. Engines, waves, equipment -- without clear communication protocols, critical information gets lost. Radio discipline exists for a reason.

### Keep It Clear and Simple

Use call-and-response format. For example:

- **You:** "Bridge, Survey."
- **Bridge:** "Bridge, go ahead."
- **You:** "Request permission to deploy equipment at the stern."
- **Bridge:** "Bridge, understood, you may deploy."
- **You:** "Survey, copy."

It might feel formal at first, but this structure confirms who is speaking, who is being spoken to, and what's being asked. It ensures the message is received and acknowledged -- not lost in radio static or misheard over engine noise.

### Use the Right Terms

Standard nautical and survey terminology removes ambiguity. Everyone knows "aft" means toward the stern and "port" means left. Using the correct terms prevents miscommunication that could lead to safety incidents or costly mistakes.

## Teamwork

Every role on a survey vessel depends on the others. Surveyors, ROV pilots, data processors, and deck crew all need to work in sync. When they do, operations run smoothly. When they don't, things slow down or go wrong.

### Know Each Other's Roles

Understanding what other team members need makes you more useful. If you know the ROV pilot needs the launch and recovery system prepped before a dive, you can anticipate that. If the data processor expects files in a particular folder structure, set it up that way from the start. Small things like this save time and reduce friction.

- See the ROV technician struggling with wiring? Grab some zip ties or pass them a tool. It takes a minute and it builds trust.
- Know how the data processor likes their files organized? Set it up that way without being asked. It saves back-and-forth later.

Ask questions about what others do. Most people are happy to explain their work, and it gives you a much better understanding of the full operation.

### Offer Help, Ask for Help

If someone's wrestling with heavy gear or stuck on a problem, help out. If you're stuck, ask. Nobody expects you to know everything when you're starting out. Trust forms when people know they can count on each other.

### Watch and Learn

As a beginner, observe how senior team members handle tasks. Much of this job is about patterns -- the sequence for powering equipment up and down, how the ROV gets positioned to avoid tangling its tether, which checks happen before a dive. Pay attention and you'll pick it up faster than you expect.

### Keep It Professional

Good morale matters on a vessel where you're living and working with the same people for weeks. A well-timed joke helps. Humor that puts someone down or distracts from safety does not.

### Respect the Chain of Command

The Party Chief or Offshore Manager sets priorities and makes the calls. That doesn't mean you can't suggest improvements -- but do it respectfully, in the right setting. Constructive feedback is welcome; public confrontations are not. Keep in mind the client may be nearby, so stay professional.

Teamwork on a vessel isn't optional. It's the backbone of every successful offshore operation.

## The Offshore Routine

Offshore life has its own rhythm. Work, rest, meals -- all structured around the operation and the shift pattern. Here's what helps:

### Stick to the Schedule

You'll likely work 12-hour shifts. Protect your sleep. If you're on days, don't stay up late in the mess hall. If you're on nights, get blackout curtains or an eye mask. Consistent sleep makes everything else easier.

### Eat Well and Hydrate

Offshore galleys serve big meals. Don't overdo it on fried food -- feeling sluggish doesn't help when you need to stay sharp. Drink water. The air conditioning dries you out, and dehydration causes headaches.

### Use Your Downtime

Read, watch something, socialize, or just have some quiet time. Find what works for you. Downtime is part of the job -- use it to recharge, not just kill time.

The people who settle into offshore life quickest are the ones who find their routine and stick to it.
