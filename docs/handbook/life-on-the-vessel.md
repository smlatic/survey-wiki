# Chapter 2: Life on the Vessel

## Vessel Terminology: Bow, Stern, and More Words You'll Hear Every Day

Offshore vessels have their own language. Before you find yourself confused in a conversation ("Wait, which side is port again?"), let's break it down. Knowing these terms doesn't just help you sound like you've been at sea for years, it also keeps you safe and efficient in day-to-day work.

- **Bow:** The front of the vessel. Think "bow" as in "leading," because it leads the way forward.
- **Stern:** The rear end. If you can see the propellers or feel the engine vibrations, you're likely near the stern.
- **Port:** The left side of the vessel when facing forward. Quick mnemonic: "Port" and "Left" both have four letters.
- **Starboard:** The right side of the vessel when facing forward.
- **Aft:** Generally means toward the back (stern) of the vessel.
- **Bridge:** The ship's command center, where navigation and overall control happen. You'll often hear radio calls like "Bridge, Survey here..."
- **Deck:** The floor (but calling it "floor" will give away that you're new).
- **Galley:** The ship's kitchen/dining area, sometimes also called the "mess." This is where you'll grab your meals and likely lots of coffee.
- **Cabin/Berth:** Your sleeping quarters. These can be small and cozy, but they're home for the duration of your shift at sea.
- **Bulkhead:** The walls. If someone says "Check the bulkhead," they mean the wall.
- **Head:** The bathroom. Yes, you read that right. On a ship, you don't ask where the bathroom is; you ask for the head.
- **Mess Hall:** Another term for the dining area, where the crew eats. Sometimes used interchangeably with galley, but often refers specifically to the seating/eating space.
- **Muster Station:** Your designated meeting point during drills or emergencies.

A few common phrases you'll hear:

- **"Secure It":** Tie down or lock in place. Things move around a lot at sea, so this is crucial.
- **"Heads Up":** Not just a warning, sometimes it's used to get people's attention for an announcement.
- **"Copy That":** A quick way of saying, "I heard and understood you."

Learning these terms isn't just about sounding like a seasoned mariner; it's about making sure you understand directions so you can work safely and confidently. Plus, it'll help you avoid the deer-in-the-headlights look when someone shouts, "Move that gear to the port side, then head to the galley for a quick bite."

### Online vs Offline: Two Words You'll Hear Constantly

On a survey vessel, "online" and "offline" don't mean internet connected or disconnected. They refer to two distinct roles in the survey workflow:

- **Online team:** These are the surveyors managing live, real-time operations. If you're online, you're monitoring positioning, running data acquisition, managing equipment during active survey work, and communicating directly with the bridge and ROV team. The online surveyor is the one keeping the live data flowing and making sure everything looks right as it comes in.

- **Offline team:** Data processors, geophysicists, video reviewers, and other specialists who work on data after it's been acquired. They clean, process, QC, and prepare deliverables. The offline team may be working simultaneously on the vessel, or they might be remote, operating from shore.

As a new surveyor, you'll likely start online, watching and assisting with live operations. It's the best way to learn how everything connects in real time.

## Communication Protocols: Talking So People Actually Listen

Imagine a world without walkie-talkies, radios, or headsets. On an offshore vessel, where there can be roaring waves, humming engines, and a million things happening at once, you'd be yelling your lungs out, and no one would ever hear you. Luckily, we have a set of communication protocols so information doesn't get lost.

### Keep It Clear and Simple

Instead of giving a long, complicated instruction all at once, break it down. For example:

- **You:** "Bridge, Survey."
- **Bridge:** "Bridge, go ahead."
- **You:** "Request permission to deploy equipment at the stern."
- **Bridge:** "Bridge, understood, you may deploy."
- **You:** "Survey, copy."

This call-and-response format might feel a bit formal at first, but it's there for a reason. It confirms that everyone involved knows who is speaking, who's being spoken to, and what's being asked. It also ensures that the message is received and acknowledged, not just lost in radio static or misheard over engine noise.

### Use the Right Terms

Out here, certain words carry specific meanings. Everyone knows that "aft" means toward the back and "port" means left. Using the right terms removes ambiguity and prevents the kind of miscommunication that could lead to costly mistakes or safety incidents.

## Teamwork & Collaboration: Trust, Respect, and the Occasional Practical Joke

An offshore survey crew is like a rock band: everyone's got their instrument, and you need all of them to play in sync to create a hit. The Surveyors, ROV Pilots, Data Processors, and Deck Crew all rely on each other. Without their combined effort, you're just making noise.

### Know Each Other's Roles

If you understand what the ROV Pilot needs before they dive the vehicle, you can anticipate their requests. Maybe they'll need the launch and recovery system set up or a specific tool on standby. If the Data Processor is waiting on you for clean positioning data, provide it promptly so they can start their magic and keep projects on schedule.

- If you see the ROV Technician prepping a new camera but struggling with the wiring, offer to grab some zip ties or pass them a tool. Even a small gesture can speed things up and build camaraderie.
- If you know the Data Processor likes data in a certain folder structure or format, set it up that way from the start. It saves back-and-forth questions later and helps them process faster.

Curiosity helps too. Ask questions about what others do. Most people love explaining their job, and it makes them feel valued.

### Offer Help, Ask for Help

If you see someone wrestling with a heavy cable drum or fiddling endlessly with a stubborn piece of kit, jump in and lend a hand. Likewise, if you're stuck, ask. Nobody expects you to know everything from day one. Trust forms when people realize they can count on each other.

### Be Observant, Look for Patterns

As a beginner, try to watch how senior team members handle tasks. A lot of this job is about patterns: which switches to flip pre-dive, the sequence for powering down equipment, or how they position the ROV to avoid tangling the tether. Sometimes, even a seasoned pro can miss a detail. If you catch it, you'll be a rock star.

### Keep It Light (But Professional)

Keeping a positive vibe helps everyone stay motivated. A well-timed joke can relieve tension, but remember: humor that lifts the team's spirits is great; humor that puts someone down or distracts from safety is not.

### Respect the Chain of Command

While collaboration is key, remember that the Party Chief or Offshore Manager sets the priorities and calls the big shots. That doesn't mean you can't suggest improvements, but do it respectfully, in the right setting. Constructive feedback is good; public showdowns are not. Also, be mindful that the client might be just around the corner, so keep your voice professional and your tone constructive at all times.

In short, teamwork isn't just a buzzword in the company brochure. It's the backbone of a successful offshore operation.

## Surviving the Offshore Routine: How to Sleep When the Boat Won't Stop Bouncing

Offshore life is a unique blend of work, rest, and trying not to spill your soup when the vessel hits a swell. Here are a few tips to keep your sanity intact:

### Embrace the Schedule

You'll likely be working in shifts, maybe 12 hours on, 12 off. Stick to your sleep schedule as best as you can. If you're a day-shift person, avoid staying up late in the mess hall all night; if you're on nights, get good blackout curtains or an eye mask. Consistency is your friend.

### Eat Right(ish) and Hydrate

Offshore galleys usually serve hearty meals, but it's up to you not to overdo it. Feeling sluggish from too many fried foods doesn't help when you need to stay sharp. Also, drink water. Yes, it's that simple. Staying hydrated keeps headaches at bay, especially when the air conditioning can be drying.

### Use Your Downtime Wisely

Read a book, watch a movie, chat with colleagues. Do whatever helps you unwind. Socializing is great for morale, but so is personal quiet time. Strike a balance.

Remember, life offshore isn't just about surviving; it's about thriving. With a bit of adaptability and a positive mindset, you'll find your rhythm on the high seas.
